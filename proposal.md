# Proposal

Understanding the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Threat Detection: Implications for Civil Liberties and National Security

Name: Hannah Gray					Department: Political Science

Background 
I attended a conference recently, the Billington Cybersecurity Summit in Washington D.C., and after returning home wanted to better understand what is missing in this larger AI conversation in relation to people’s civil liberties and defense. After speaking with many people, it’s very clear AI is becoming more important for threat detection online by both private companies and the government. Their systems are looking through huge datasets made up of online communications, facial analysis, behavior, and everything in between in order to find malicious players ahead of time. The topic at hand is now, while this is great, impact on privacy for the everyday person. 

Recently, even with an LLM like ChatGPT for example, AI models are becoming proficient at noticing trends. This is great for personal day-to-day use in the context of what coffee or restaurant someone may like, but in a defense context may largely point out criminal behavior. Conversely, AI is not perfect yet and may find a pattern that is not actually a threat or toes the line on invasive vs not. Likewise, there is a sense in the air of people feeling as if they can’t do anything without being watched by ‘big brother’ or AI. 

The goal here is to better understand the balance and people’s appetite for national security benefits vs privacy rights, relative to AI systems. Specifically this means: 

1. Understand in detail how the government is currently using these systems
2. If these systems do in reality introduce major risks to violating people’s privacy
3. What the current public perception of AI is in relation to privacy vs defense

Getting the answer to these three questions I believe is enough to demonstrate what people are willing to tolerate when it comes to using AI systems for national security. 

Methodology 
Gathering this data will require a few different means of doing so, using both qualitative and quantitative collection. 

First, I’ll start by gathering and reviewing existing material to understand what specific AI systems are currently in play, their capabilities, and the pros/cons associated with each system that’s being used. This will be anything from government reports, industry handbooks, academic articles, and courses. 

Next, I will require a survey that will capture input from 50 selected participants to gauge their feelings on AI in relation to national security defense. This survey will involve questions looking to understand if the participants know the risks/benefits and if so, are those risks/benefits worth it to them. Likewise, it will also seek to establish a baseline on their general knowledge of AI systems and their trust in them. 

Finally, in the last phase I interview people firsthand to get a close up look as to why they feel the way they do about AI (good or bad), if that sense will change in the future, their proposed solutions and desired outcomes, and what sort of accountability system they would want, if any. 

Once all three steps are completed I’ll look through the data, combine it all, and present my findings.

Anticipated Outcomes - Report
The ultimate goal is to show what the everyday person thinks of AI in relation to national security and their own privacy and what their tolerance is for AI systems at the cost of potentially crossing privacy lines. My assumption is that the gap between ‘industry experts’ and the day to day person is as wide as the Grand Canyon. This may uncover that more accountability is needed for the government and people implementing these systems to their tooling. 

Significance 
The work done in this research is critical to defending privacy lines for people. Ultimately, the hope is that it will be used to create industry benchmarks and accountability systems. 
Policy makers, professors, students, AI companies, will all benefit from this as it will be the groundwork for accountability.  
The knowledge to be gained here is that both sides of the aisle, AI experts and everyday citizens, will see how wide the gap is in terms of what’s okay relative to AI and the door will be opened for the two to understand what each side wants.
